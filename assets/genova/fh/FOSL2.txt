# FOSL2

## Overview
FOSL2, or FOS-like antigen 2, is a gene that encodes a protein component of the AP-1 transcription factor complex, known as FOS like 2, AP-1 transcription factor subunit. This protein is characterized by a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) domain, which is essential for its dimerization and DNA-binding capabilities, allowing it to regulate gene expression in response to various stimuli (Wrann2012FOSL2). FOSL2 plays a significant role in numerous cellular processes, including proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis, by modulating the transcription of target genes. It is particularly involved in the regulation of leptin expression in adipocytes and the modulation of Th17 cell responses in the immune system (Shetty2021The; Wrann2012FOSL2). Alterations in FOSL2 expression or function have been linked to autoimmune and inflammatory diseases, as well as metabolic disorders, highlighting its clinical significance (Wrann2012FOSL2; Renoux2020The).

## Structure
FOSL2, also known as FOS-like antigen 2, is a protein that is part of the AP-1 transcription factor complex. The protein contains a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) domain, which is crucial for its function. This domain facilitates dimerization and DNA binding, allowing FOSL2 to regulate gene expression in response to various stimuli. The bZIP domain is characterized by a leucine zipper motif that enables the formation of dimers, which is essential for its interaction with DNA.

## Function
FOSL2, also known as FOS-like antigen 2, is a component of the AP-1 transcription factor family, which includes proteins such as FOS, FOSB, FOSL1, JUN, JUNB, and JUND. These transcription factors are characterized by a basic leucine zipper (bZIP) domain and require dimerization to bind DNA (Wrann2012FOSL2). FOSL2 plays a crucial role in regulating gene expression in response to various stimuli, including growth factors and stress, and is involved in cellular processes such as proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis.

In adipocytes, FOSL2 is a key transcriptional regulator of the leptin (LEP) gene, promoting its expression by binding to a specific cis-regulatory element upstream of the LEP transcription start site. This binding is crucial for adipocyte-specific LEP expression, and FOSL2's role is particularly significant during the early stages of adipocyte differentiation (Wrann2012FOSL2). FOSL2 expression is upregulated during adipogenesis and in conditions with increased leptin levels, such as in obesity (Wrann2012FOSL2).

In the immune system, FOSL2, along with FOSL1, regulates Th17 cell responses. It acts as a repressor of Th17 lineage, influencing the expression of key Th17-marker genes and modulating immune responses. FOSL2's interactions with other proteins suggest its involvement in post-transcriptional gene regulation and monitoring Th17 responses (Shetty2021Mapping; Shetty2021The).

## Clinical Significance
Alterations in the expression or function of the FOSL2 gene have been implicated in various autoimmune and inflammatory diseases. Overexpression of FOSL2 in mice leads to systemic autoimmunity and inflammation, characterized by a decrease in regulatory T cell (Treg) populations, which are crucial for maintaining immune tolerance. This reduction in Tregs contributes to inflammatory conditions such as colitis and lung inflammation (Renoux2020The). FOSL2 represses the expression of FoxP3, a key transcription factor for Treg development, leading to a phenotype similar to Treg-deficient conditions (Renoux2020The).

Genome-wide association studies have linked single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) within the promoter of FOSL2 to an increased risk of inflammatory diseases, including Crohn's disease, ulcerative colitis, asthma, hay fever, and eczema (Renoux2020The). SNPs within FOSL2 binding sites are also associated with autoimmune conditions such as multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, and ankylosing spondylitis, potentially affecting Th17 signaling pathways (Shetty2021The).

In the context of metabolic disorders, FOSL2 is involved in regulating leptin expression in adipocytes. Alterations in FOSL2 expression can impact leptin levels, which may influence conditions related to leptin, such as obesity (Wrann2012FOSL2).

## Interactions
FOSL2, a member of the AP-1 transcription factor family, engages in various interactions with proteins and nucleic acids, playing a significant role in cellular processes. In human Th17 cells, FOSL2 interacts with several proteins, including JUNB, SIRT-1, RUNX1, and EIF4E, which are crucial for Th17 lineage-associated functions. These interactions suggest FOSL2's involvement in modulating Th17 cell effector responses, with its interaction with JUNB promoting Th17 fate and interactions with RUNX1 and SIRT-1 influencing Th17 cell signaling (Shetty2021Mapping).

FOSL2 also interacts with eukaryotic translational initiation factors such as eIF4G1, eIF4E, eIF3I, and eIF2AK2, indicating its role in the regulation of translational initiation (Shetty2021Mapping). Additionally, FOSL2 forms heterodimers with JUN family proteins, which is essential for its DNA-binding activity and transcriptional regulation (Renoux2020The).

In adipocytes, FOSL2 binds to the enhancer element UpE, crucial for leptin gene expression, and interacts with nucleoproteins identified through proteome-wide screening (Wrann2012FOSL2). These interactions highlight FOSL2's role in regulating gene expression through direct binding to DNA and forming complexes with other proteins.
