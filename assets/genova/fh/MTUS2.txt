# MTUS2

## Overview
MTUS2, or microtubule associated scaffold protein 2, is a gene that encodes a protein integral to cellular processes involving the cytoskeleton and microtubule dynamics. The protein, categorized as a scaffold protein, plays a pivotal role in maintaining cell structure and facilitating intracellular signaling. It is predominantly active in the cytoplasm, where it stabilizes microtubules, essential components of the cytoskeleton that provide structural support and enable intracellular transport. MTUS2's involvement in these processes underscores its significance in cell division, stability, and communication, influencing pathways that affect cell growth and differentiation. Additionally, MTUS2 has been implicated in various disease contexts, including cancer and reproductive health disorders, highlighting its potential as a target for therapeutic interventions (Xu2024N6methyladenosine; Vastrad2023The).

## Interactions
MTUS2, also known as microtubule associated scaffold protein 2, is involved in various cellular processes through its interactions with other proteins and nucleic acids. In hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), MTUS2 expression is regulated by the circular RNA circASH2L, which acts as a competing endogenous RNA (ceRNA) by sponging miR-525-3p. This interaction prevents miR-525-3p from suppressing MTUS2 expression, thereby promoting its tumor suppressor role. The study demonstrated that circASH2L impedes the interaction between MTUS2 and miR-525-3p, leading to increased MTUS2 expression, which is associated with reduced proliferation, migration, and invasion of HCC cells (Xu2024N6methyladenosine).

MTUS2 is also identified as a hub gene in the context of Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS), suggesting its potential involvement in protein-protein interaction networks. However, specific interactions of MTUS2 with other proteins or pathways in PCOS are not detailed (Vastrad2023The).

These interactions highlight the role of MTUS2 in cellular signaling pathways and its potential impact on disease mechanisms, particularly in cancer and reproductive health contexts. Understanding these interactions can provide insights into its functional roles in cellular dynamics.
