# RAB15

## Overview
RAB15 is a gene that encodes a member of the Rab GTPase family, specifically the RAB15 protein, which is part of the RAS oncogene family. This protein is primarily involved in intracellular vesicle trafficking, a critical cellular process that ensures the proper transport and sorting of cargo within cells. RAB15 is predominantly localized to early endosomes and plays a significant role in regulating the transport of cargo from sorting endosomes to either degradative or recycling pathways. It is also involved in the transport of cargoes from apical recycling endosomes to basolateral membranes, highlighting its importance in maintaining cellular homeostasis and function (Hutagalung2011Role; Bhuin2014Rab). The protein interacts with other Rab proteins and effectors, such as Rep15, to modulate vesicle trafficking and endocytosis, processes that are crucial for nutrient uptake and receptor recycling (Yang2024Ehrlichia; Rai2022Rep15). Additionally, RAB15 has been implicated in various diseases, including colorectal cancer, where its altered expression may influence tumor progression and prognosis (Jiang2022The).

## Function
RAB15 is a member of the Rab GTPase family, which plays a critical role in intracellular vesicle trafficking. In healthy human cells, RAB15 is predominantly localized to early endosomes and is involved in regulating the transport of cargo from sorting endosomes to either degradative or recycling pathways (Bhuin2014Rab). It is also associated with the transport of cargoes from apical recycling endosomes to basolateral membranes, indicating its role in sorting and directing vesicular traffic within cells (Bhuin2014Rab).

RAB15 co-localizes with other Rab proteins such as Rab4, Rab5, and Rab11 on early endosomes and the endocytic recycling compartment, suggesting its involvement in distinct roles across different endocytic compartments (Rai2022Rep15). It is known to inhibit fluid phase and receptor-mediated endocytosis without affecting recycling rates from early endosomal compartments, indicating a regulatory role in endocytosis (Bhuin2014Rab).

The protein is active in the cytoplasm and associated with endosomal membranes, contributing to essential cellular processes like nutrient uptake and receptor recycling. This activity is crucial for maintaining cellular homeostasis and function, particularly in pathways involving endosomes and recycling processes (Hutagalung2011Role).

## Clinical Significance
RAB15, a member of the RAS oncogene family, has been implicated in various diseases due to its altered expression and interactions. In colorectal cancer (CRC), RAB15 is significantly upregulated in tumor tissues compared to normal tissues, suggesting a potential role in tumor progression and prognosis. This upregulation is associated with the metabolic activated CRC subtype (CMS3) and is linked to cell cycle-related pathways and mismatch repair (MMR) genes, indicating that RAB15 may influence cell metabolism and tumor growth in CRC (Jiang2022The).

The dysregulation of RAB15, along with other Rab GTPases, can lead to impaired vesicle trafficking, contributing to tumorigenesis. Rab proteins, including RAB15, function as molecular switches in vesicle transport, and their dysregulation can affect cancer cell behaviors such as uncontrolled growth, invasion, and metastasis (Tzeng2016Rabmediated). Although specific mutation frequencies for RAB15 in CRC are not detailed, its altered expression may impact clinical outcomes and treatment responses (Jiang2022The).

In neuronal development, RAB15 is involved in transferrin receptor trafficking, but its specific clinical implications in neurological conditions remain unclear, highlighting the need for further research (D’Adamo2014RAB).

## Interactions
RAB15, a member of the RAS oncogene family, is involved in intracellular vesicle trafficking and interacts with several proteins, playing a crucial role in various cellular processes. One significant interaction is with Rep15, an effector molecule that forms a stable complex with RAB15. This interaction has been characterized using biochemical techniques such as analytical size exclusion chromatography and isothermal titration calorimetry, revealing a dissociation constant of 0.47 ± 0.12 µM, indicating a strong binding affinity (Rai2022Rep15). Rep15 is involved in receptor recycling from the endocytic recycling compartment and co-localizes with RAB15 on endosomal membranes (Rai2022Rep15).

RAB15 also interacts with RAB5 on the Ehrlichia chaffeensis-containing vesicle (ECV) membrane. This interaction is crucial in inhibiting the maturation of ECVs to late endosomes, as RAB15 counteracts the effects of RAB5, potentially by sequestering RAB5 effectors or through other interactions. This inhibition prevents the recruitment of RAB7, which is necessary for ECV maturation (Yang2024Ehrlichia). These interactions highlight RAB15's role in modulating vesicle trafficking and autophagy, processes that are exploited by pathogens like Ehrlichia chaffeensis for intracellular growth (Yang2024Ehrlichia).
