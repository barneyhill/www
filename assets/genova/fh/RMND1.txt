# RMND1

## Overview
The RMND1 gene encodes a protein that is crucial for mitochondrial function, particularly in the context of mitochondrial translation and the assembly of oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) complexes. The RMND1 protein, categorized as a mitochondrial membrane protein, plays a pivotal role in the translation of mitochondrial DNA-encoded polypeptides, which are essential components of the OXPHOS system. This protein is integral to the inner mitochondrial membrane and is involved in forming a large complex that supports the translation process by anchoring or stabilizing the mitochondrial ribosome near mRNA maturation sites. Mutations in the RMND1 gene can lead to severe clinical conditions, including Combined Oxidative Phosphorylation Deficiency 11 (COXPD11) and Perrault-like syndrome, characterized by a range of symptoms such as sensorineural hearing loss, neurological deficits, and metabolic abnormalities (Ng2016The; Janer2015RMND1).

## Function
The RMND1 gene encodes a protein that plays a critical role in mitochondrial function, particularly in the assembly and maintenance of the oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) complexes. In healthy human cells, RMND1 is essential for the proper assembly of complexes I, III, IV, and V of the OXPHOS system, which are crucial for mitochondrial energy production (Janer2015RMND1). The RMND1 protein is an integral component of the inner mitochondrial membrane and forms a large 240 kDa complex that supports the translation of the 13 polypeptides encoded by mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA). These polypeptides are vital subunits of the OXPHOS complexes (Janer2015RMND1).

RMND1 is hypothesized to anchor or stabilize the mitochondrial ribosome near sites where mRNAs are matured, thereby spatially coupling the post-transcriptional handling of mRNAs with their translation. This function is critical for the proper assembly and function of the OXPHOS complexes, which are necessary for cellular energy production (Ng2016The). The protein localizes to discrete foci in the mitochondrial network, juxtaposed to RNA granules where primary mitochondrial transcripts are processed, highlighting its role in maintaining mitochondrial ribosome integrity and function (Janer2015RMND1).

## Clinical Significance
Mutations in the RMND1 gene are associated with a range of severe clinical phenotypes due to defects in mitochondrial translation. These mutations can lead to conditions such as Combined Oxidative Phosphorylation Deficiency 11 (COXPD11), characterized by neonatal encephaloneuromyopathy, sensorineural hearing loss, cardiac and kidney involvement, neurological symptoms, and elevated lactate levels (Bayrak2023Expanding). Clinical manifestations of RMND1 mutations include congenital sensorineural deafness, hypotonia, lactic acidaemia, and multiple mitochondrial respiratory chain deficiencies (Gaboon2020Exome). 

Specific mutations, such as c.713A>G (p.Asn238Ser), are linked to Perrault-like syndrome, which includes sensorineural hearing loss, primary ovarian insufficiency, renal dysfunction, and short stature (Demain2018A). Another mutation, c.631G > A (p.Val211Met), has been associated with chronic kidney disease and hearing loss, and is linked to defects in sphingolipid metabolism, potentially leading to apoptosis and tissue damage (Gaboon2020Exome). 

RMND1 mutations can also result in severe renal hypoplasia and extrarenal abnormalities, such as sensorineural deafness and developmental delays, as observed in cases with compound heterozygous mutations (Broenen2019RMND1). These mutations often result in early fatal outcomes due to severe encephalopathy and lactic acidosis (Broenen2019RMND1).

## Interactions
The RMND1 protein is integral to mitochondrial function, primarily through its role in mitochondrial translation. It forms a large 240 kDa complex within the inner mitochondrial membrane, which is crucial for the translation of the 13 polypeptides encoded by mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) (Janer2015RMND1). RMND1 interacts with mitochondrial ribosomal proteins, such as MRPL11 and MRPL12, suggesting its involvement in the assembly or stabilization of the mitochondrial ribosome (Janer2015RMND1). This interaction is essential for the proper assembly of oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) complexes, as RMND1 deficiency leads to defects in the assembly of complexes I, III, IV, and V (Janer2015RMND1).

RMND1 is also suggested to anchor or stabilize the mitochondrial ribosome near sites of mRNA maturation, thereby spatially coupling post-transcriptional mRNA handling with translation (Ng2016The). This indicates a potential interaction with RNA granules, which are involved in the processing of primary mitochondrial transcripts (Janer2015RMND1). The absence of RMND1 foci in patient fibroblasts further supports its role in the spatial organization of mitochondrial translation (Janer2015RMND1). These interactions highlight RMND1's critical function in maintaining mitochondrial translation and respiratory chain complex formation.
