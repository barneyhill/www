# CCDC71L

## Overview
The CCDC71L gene encodes the coiled-coil domain containing 71 like protein, which is involved in various cellular processes. This protein is characterized by the presence of coiled-coil domains, which are structural motifs that facilitate protein-protein interactions. While the precise biological functions of CCDC71L remain to be fully elucidated, it has been implicated in the regulation of cellular proliferation and migration, particularly in the context of cancer. Research has shown that CCDC71L is highly expressed in certain cancer types, such as triple-negative breast cancer, where it is regulated by non-coding RNAs and microRNAs (Luo2020LINC00514; Luo2021LINC00514). Additionally, genetic variants of CCDC71L have been associated with cardiovascular traits, suggesting a broader role in human health and disease (Bis2014Sequencing; Franceschini2018GWAS).

## Clinical Significance
The CCDC71L gene has been implicated in several diseases and conditions due to mutations, alterations in expression levels, or changes in its interactions. In the context of cardiovascular diseases, genetic variants in CCDC71L have been associated with subclinical atherosclerosis traits, such as carotid intima-media thickness (cIMT) and carotid plaque. A specific variant, rs2286149, located in the 3ʹ UTR of CCDC71L, shows significant association with these traits, suggesting a role in the genetic architecture of atherosclerosis (Bis2014Sequencing; Franceschini2018GWAS).

In cancer research, CCDC71L is highly expressed in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) cells. It is regulated by the long non-coding RNA LINC00514, which promotes TNBC progression by sponging microRNAs miR-6504-5p and miR-3139. These microRNAs typically inhibit CCDC71L, and their suppression leads to increased cell proliferation, migration, and invasion, while reducing apoptosis in TNBC cells (Luo2020LINC00514; Luo2021LINC00514).

These findings highlight the potential of CCDC71L as a biomarker or therapeutic target in both cardiovascular and cancer-related conditions, although further research is needed to fully elucidate its clinical significance.
