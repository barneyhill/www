# CST8

## Overview
CST8 is a gene that encodes the protein cystatin 8, a member of the cystatin superfamily known for its role as a protease inhibitor. Unlike other cystatins that primarily inhibit cysteine proteases, cystatin 8 uniquely inhibits serine proteases, such as prohormone convertase 2 and 4, suggesting its involvement in proprotein processing. This protein is predominantly expressed in the male reproductive system, particularly in the testis and epididymis, where it plays a crucial role in spermatogenesis and sperm maturation. Cystatin 8 is also noted for its antimicrobial activity and its ability to form amyloid structures, which may contribute to sperm maturation. The absence of CST8 in mice leads to fertility issues and an age-dependent lysosomal storage disease-like phenotype, underscoring its importance in reproductive health (Whelly2016Cystatinrelated).

## Function
CST8, also known as cystatin 8, is a protein primarily expressed in the male reproductive system, particularly in the testis and epididymis. It is a member of the cystatin superfamily, which generally functions as inhibitors of cysteine proteases. However, CST8 is unique in that it does not inhibit cysteine proteases but instead inhibits serine proteases such as prohormone convertase 2 and 4. This suggests a role in regulating proprotein processing (Whelly2016Cystatinrelated).

In the testis, CST8 is expressed in spermatids and is present in the sperm acrosome and tail, indicating its involvement in spermatogenesis and sperm maturation. In the epididymis, CST8 is secreted into the lumen by principal cells and is associated with maturing spermatozoa, suggesting a protective function against pathogens through its antimicrobial activity (Whelly2016Cystatinrelated). CST8 is also aggregation-prone, forming amyloid structures in the epididymal lumen, which may play a functional role in sperm maturation (Whelly2016Cystatinrelated).

Mice lacking the CST8 gene exhibit fertility defects due to impaired acrosome reaction and develop an age-dependent lysosomal storage disease-like phenotype in the epididymis, highlighting its critical role in reproductive health (Whelly2016Cystatinrelated).
