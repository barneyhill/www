# TBX22

## Overview
TBX22 is a gene that encodes the T-box transcription factor 22, a member of the T-box family of transcription factors, which are characterized by a conserved DNA-binding domain known as the T-box. This transcription factor plays a pivotal role in craniofacial development, particularly in the formation of the palate and tongue. The TBX22 protein functions primarily as a transcriptional repressor, modulating the expression of genes involved in embryogenesis and tissue differentiation. It is regulated by signaling pathways such as FGF and BMP, which are crucial for its activity during facial and palatal development. Mutations in the TBX22 gene are linked to craniofacial anomalies, including X-linked cleft palate and ankyloglossia, highlighting its clinical significance in developmental disorders (Pauws2009Tbx22; Bush2002Isolation; Fuchs2010Regulation).

## Structure
The TBX22 protein is a member of the T-box family of transcription factors, characterized by a conserved DNA-binding domain known as the T-box, which is approximately 200 amino acids long (Herr2003Expression). This domain is crucial for the protein's function in transcriptional regulation. The human TBX22 protein shares significant sequence identity and similarity with its mouse counterpart, particularly within the T-box domain, which is 88% identical and 92% similar (Herr2003Expression).

The primary structure of TBX22 includes specific amino acid sequences that determine its function, with the full-length protein comprising 517 amino acids (Herr2003Expression). ## Function
The TBX22 gene encodes a T-box transcription factor that plays a critical role in craniofacial development, particularly in the formation of the palate and tongue. In healthy human cells, TBX22 is involved in the regulation of gene expression during embryogenesis, influencing cell differentiation and tissue development. It is active in the nucleus, where it binds to DNA to modulate the transcription of target genes (Bush2002Isolation).

TBX22 is regulated by FGF and BMP signaling pathways, with FGF stimulating and BMP inhibiting its expression. This regulation is crucial for its role in facial and palatal development (Fuchs2010Regulation). The gene acts as a transcriptional repressor and is involved in the development of the frontonasal region, the first branchial arch, and subsequently the palate and base of the tongue (Fuchs2010Regulation).

In terms of molecular processes, TBX22 is important for the maturation of osteoblasts and the ossification process in the palate, which is essential for normal craniofacial development. Its absence can lead to defects such as submucous cleft palate and ankyloglossia, highlighting its role in maintaining the structural integrity and function of craniofacial regions (Pauws2009Tbx22).

## Clinical Significance
Mutations in the TBX22 gene are associated with several craniofacial anomalies, most notably X-linked cleft palate and ankyloglossia (CPX). These conditions arise due to disruptions in the gene's normal function, which is crucial for craniofacial development. The TBX22 gene is located on the X chromosome, and mutations can lead to a range of phenotypes, including cleft palate, bifid uvula, and ankyloglossia, with varying severity among affected individuals (Slavec2023A; Pauws2009Tbx22).

A specific loss-of-function mutation in the TBX22 promoter, identified as -73G>A, disrupts an ETS-1 binding site, leading to decreased transcriptional activity and is linked to cleft palate only (CPO) in hemizygous males (Fu2014Lossoffunction). Other mutations, such as splicing site mutations and missense mutations, have been identified in different populations and are associated with nonsyndromic cleft lip/palate (NSCL/P) (Dai2018Novel; Basharat2022Two).

In addition to cleft palate, TBX22 mutations can result in ankyloglossia, a condition characterized by a tongue-tie, which can occur alone or in conjunction with cleft palate (Pauws2009Tbx22). The gene's role in palatogenesis and glossogenesis underscores its clinical significance in craniofacial disorders (Dai2018Novel).

## Interactions
TBX22 interacts with several proteins and nucleic acids, playing a significant role in transcriptional regulation during development. One of the key interactions is with the transcription factor ETS-1. The TBX22 promoter contains an ETS-1 binding site, which is crucial for its transcriptional activity. A mutation in this site, specifically -73G>A, disrupts the binding of ETS-1, leading to reduced promoter activity and is associated with cleft palate in hemizygous males (Fu2014Lossoffunction).

TBX22 also interacts with SUMO-1, a small ubiquitin-like modifier involved in various cellular processes, including craniofacial development. This interaction affects the sumoylation of TBX22, which is important for its ability to recognize target DNA. Missense mutations in TBX22 can impair its sumoylation, impacting its function (Fu2014Lossoffunction).

In avian models, TBX22 acts as a repressor for the expression of DLX5 and MSX2, genes involved in facial development. This repression is part of its role in regulating frontonasal morphogenesis (Higashihori2010The). TBX22 is also influenced by signaling pathways such as BMP and FGF, which modulate its expression and activity during development (Higashihori2010The; Fuchs2010Regulation).
