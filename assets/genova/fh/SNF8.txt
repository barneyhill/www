# SNF8

## Overview
SNF8 is a gene that encodes the SNF8 protein, a subunit of the Endosomal Sorting Complex Required for Transport II (ESCRT-II). This protein is integral to the ESCRT machinery, which is involved in the sorting and trafficking of ubiquitinated membrane proteins to lysosomes for degradation, a process crucial for cellular homeostasis and membrane protein turnover (Carrasquillo2012SNF8). The SNF8 protein contains a Vps22/25 domain, facilitating its interaction with other ESCRT-II components, and plays a role in membrane budding and scission (Hurley2010Membrane). Beyond its traditional role in endosomal sorting, SNF8 is implicated in modulating TRPC6 channel activity and participating in unconventional secretion pathways, highlighting its diverse functional roles in cellular processes (Carrasquillo2012SNF8). Additionally, SNF8 is involved in antiviral defense mechanisms, contributing to the activation of interferon-stimulated genes (Kumthip2017Pivotal).

SNF8 contains a Vps22/25 domain, which is critical for its function within the ESCRT-II complex. This domain facilitates interactions with other subunits of the complex, such as Vps22 and Vps36, forming a rigid Y-shaped core structure (Hurley2010Membrane). The quaternary structure of SNF8 involves its assembly with other ESCRT-II components, contributing to the complex's role in membrane budding and scission (Hurley2010Membrane).

## Function
SNF8, a component of the ESCRT-II complex, plays a crucial role in the endosomal sorting of ubiquitinated membrane proteins, facilitating their trafficking and degradation within lysosomes. This process is essential for maintaining cellular homeostasis and regulating membrane protein turnover. SNF8 is active in the cytoplasm, particularly within endosomal compartments, where it contributes to the formation of multivesicular bodies (MVBs) (Carrasquillo2012SNF8).

In addition to its role in endosomal sorting, SNF8 has been identified as a modulator of TRPC6 channel activity. It enhances TRPC6-mediated currents and downstream NFAT-mediated transcription, although it does not alter the surface expression of TRPC6. This suggests that SNF8 may influence TRPC6 activity through mechanisms other than changes in its localization or expression levels (Carrasquillo2012SNF8).

SNF8 is also involved in the unconventional secretion pathway of certain proteins, such as CFTRΔF508, through its participation in the MVB pathway. Knockdown of SNF8 significantly reduces the rescue of mutant CFTR, indicating its role in this process (Noh2018Specific). These functions highlight SNF8's diverse roles in cellular processes beyond its traditional involvement in the ESCRT pathway.

## Clinical Significance
Mutations and alterations in the expression of the SNF8 gene, a component of the ESCRT-II complex, have been implicated in various diseases. SNF8 is involved in calcium signaling through its interaction with TRPC6, a channel associated with conditions such as focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), cardiac hypertrophy, and pulmonary hypertension. Overexpression of SNF8 enhances TRPC6-mediated currents and NFAT-mediated transcription, suggesting a role in modulating TRPC6 activity, which could be relevant for therapeutic strategies targeting TRPC6-related pathologies (Carrasquillo2012SNF8).

SNF8 is also associated with coronary artery disease (CAD). It is upregulated in smooth muscle cells from atherosclerotic mice and linked to risk alleles in human aortic endothelial cells, indicating its involvement in disease progression (Erbilgin2013Identification). In the context of antiviral defense, SNF8, also known as EAP30, is crucial for the induction of type I and III interferons and interferon-stimulated genes, playing a pivotal role in IRF3-dependent innate immune responses (Kumthip2017Pivotal).

In C. elegans, the homologous gene vps-22/snf8 is involved in regulating longevity through its interaction with the daf-16 gene, suggesting potential implications for aging-related processes in humans (Han2020VPS22SNF8).

## Interactions
SNF8, a component of the ESCRT-II complex, participates in several protein interactions that are crucial for its function. It interacts with TRPC6, a channel protein, enhancing TRPC6-mediated currents and NFAT-mediated transcription. This interaction is specific to the amino-terminal 107 amino acids of TRPC6 and was confirmed through yeast two-hybrid assays and co-immunoprecipitation (Carrasquillo2012SNF8). SNF8's interaction with TRPC6 does not alter the channel's surface expression, suggesting a regulatory role in channel activity rather than trafficking (Carrasquillo2012SNF8).

In the context of antiviral defense, SNF8, also known as EAP30, forms a complex with IRF3 and its transcriptional co-activator CBP in the nucleus. This complex is essential for the binding of IRF3 to target gene promoters, such as IFN-β, IFNL1, and IFIT1, facilitating the antiviral response (Kumthip2017Pivotal). The interaction between SNF8, IRF3, and CBP is enhanced following viral infection, indicating a role in the formation of an 'enhanceosome' that activates antiviral gene promoters (Kumthip2017Pivotal). These interactions highlight SNF8's involvement in both membrane trafficking and immune response regulation.
