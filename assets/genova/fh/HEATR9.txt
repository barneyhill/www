## Structure
The HEATR9 protein, encoded by the HEATR9 gene, is characterized by the presence of HEAT repeats, which are structural motifs consisting of two amphiphilic alpha helices connected by an intra-unit loop. These repeats form a solenoid structure, with a hydrophobic core and hydrophilic exterior, facilitating protein-protein interactions (Stairiker2020Heatr9; Omolaoye2022Using). The HEAT repeats are a common feature in proteins involved in various cellular processes, such as Huntingtin and protein phosphatase 2A, indicating a potential role in mediating interactions with other proteins (Stairiker2020Heatr9).

While the specific primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary structures of HEATR9 are not detailed in the available literature, the presence of HEAT repeats suggests a conserved tertiary structure typical of proteins with these motifs (Stairiker2020Heatr9). The protein's function during viral infections, such as influenza and RSV, is linked to its role in cytokine expression and inflammation, although the exact mechanisms remain to be fully elucidated (Stairiker2020Heatr9). ## Clinical Significance
The HEATR9 gene has been implicated in various clinical contexts, particularly in relation to viral infections and immune response. During influenza virus infection, HEATR9 is significantly upregulated in alveolar epithelial cells, suggesting a role in the host's immune response. It appears to regulate the expression of chemokines such as Ccl5 and Ccl4, which are crucial for immune cell recruitment. Knockdown experiments have shown that reducing HEATR9 expression leads to decreased transcription and secretion of these chemokines, indicating its regulatory role during infection (Stairiker2024Molecular).

HEATR9 expression is also associated with optimal influenza virus production, suggesting a potential role in viral replication (Stairiker2024Molecular). In the context of COVID-19, decreased expression of HEATR9 in whole blood has been linked to an impaired immune response, highlighting its potential involvement in susceptibility and progression of the disease (Patil2021COVID19).

Although specific diseases directly caused by mutations in HEATR9 are not well-documented, its expression levels and interactions with other genes, such as Ccl5, suggest that alterations in HEATR9 could impact conditions related to immune response and inflammation, such as vascular damage and nephropathy (Stairiker2024Molecular).
