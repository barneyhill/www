# TOMM22

## Overview
TOMM22 is a gene that encodes the translocase of outer mitochondrial membrane 22 (Tom22), a critical component of the translocase of the outer mitochondrial membrane (TOM) complex. This protein is a single-pass transmembrane receptor that plays a pivotal role in the import of precursor proteins into mitochondria, a process essential for mitochondrial function and cellular energy production (Heinemeyer2019Underappreciated). Tom22 is involved in various cellular processes, including apoptosis and mitochondrial quality control, and interacts with several other proteins within the TOM complex to facilitate the translocation of proteins across the mitochondrial membranes (Pitt2021A; Yang2023TOMM40). The gene's expression and function have significant implications in health and disease, particularly in conditions involving mitochondrial dysfunction, such as neurodegenerative diseases and cardiovascular health (Gialluisi2021Identification; Heinemeyer2019Underappreciated).

## Structure
TOMM22 is a key component of the translocase of the outer mitochondrial membrane (TOM) complex, which is essential for the import of proteins into mitochondria. The protein is a single-pass transmembrane protein with a specific structure that includes an N-terminal cis domain exposed to the cytosol, a transmembrane domain, and a C-terminal trans domain located in the intermembrane space (IMS) (Perry2008Structure). The cis domain is involved in binding N-terminal targeting presequences, while the trans domain helps hold these presequences in the IMS after translocation (Perry2008Structure).

The TOM complex, including TOMM22, can exist in different assembly states, such as a trimeric form containing TOMM22 and a dimeric form lacking it. These forms are thought to handle different sets of mitochondrial precursor proteins (Araiso2020Structural). In the trimeric form, TOMM22 interacts with Tom40 through salt bridges, contributing to the structural integrity of the complex (Araiso2020Structural). The transmembrane segment of TOMM22 is crucial for maintaining the structural integrity of the TOM complex, highlighting its role in the quaternary structure of the complex (Perry2008Structure).

## Function
TOMM22 encodes the Tom22 protein, a critical component of the translocase of the outer mitochondrial membrane (TOM) complex, which is essential for the import of proteins into mitochondria. Tom22 acts as a receptor, recognizing and binding precursor proteins with N-terminal presequences, and facilitates their translocation across the outer mitochondrial membrane (Heinemeyer2019Underappreciated). Approximately 1100 out of 1500 proteins imported by the TOM complex rely on Tom22, underscoring its importance in mitochondrial protein import and cellular energy production (Pitt2021A).

Tom22 is involved in various cellular processes, including apoptosis, where it interacts with the pro-apoptotic protein Bax, a significant target in cancer therapeutics (Pitt2021A). It also plays a role in mitochondrial quality control, particularly in the mitophagy pathway involving Pink1 and Parkin, which are crucial for maintaining mitochondrial function (Pitt2021A). In skeletal muscle cells, phosphorylation of Tom22 by protein kinase CSNK2/CK2 is essential for mitophagy and mitochondrial homeostasis (Kravic2018In).

Structurally, Tom22 stabilizes the Tom40 β-barrels within the TOM complex and is involved in the assembly and organization of the complex, acting as a major site for preprotein binding (Pitt2021A). Its interactions and modifications highlight its diverse roles in maintaining mitochondrial integrity and function across various tissues (Pitt2021A).

## Clinical Significance
Alterations in the expression of the TOMM22 gene have been implicated in various diseases, particularly those involving mitochondrial dysfunction. In the context of statin-induced mitochondrial dysfunction, downregulation of TOMM22, along with TOMM40, has been shown to impair mitochondrial function in skeletal muscle cells. This impairment is characterized by reduced oxygen consumption rate, decreased ATP production, and increased mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (mitoROS) production, which may contribute to statin-associated muscle symptoms (SAMS) (Yang2023TOMM40).

In neurodegenerative diseases, TOMM22 has been identified as a candidate gene for Parkinson's disease (PD). Variants in TOMM22 are more frequently found in PD patients compared to controls, suggesting a potential role in the disease's etiopathogenesis. TOMM22 is expressed in dopaminergic neurons, indicating it may influence neuronal identity, survival, or dopamine neurotransmission (Gialluisi2021Identification).

TOMM22 is also involved in cardiovascular health, where its interaction with the adenine nucleotide translocator and mitochondrial calcium-activated potassium channels suggests a role in cardiac mitochondrial calcium import and cellular survival. Disruption of these interactions could lead to inefficient cardiac cell functioning (Heinemeyer2019Underappreciated).

## Interactions
TOMM22, a component of the translocase of the outer mitochondrial membrane (TOM) complex, is involved in several critical protein interactions essential for mitochondrial function. It interacts with other TOM complex proteins, such as TOM20 and TOM40, to facilitate the import of precursor proteins into mitochondria (Yang2023TOMM40). TOMM22 also plays a role in the TOM-TIM23 supercomplex, where it interacts with Tim21, Tim23, and Tim50. These interactions are crucial for the transfer of precursor proteins from the TOM complex to the TIM23 complex across the intermembrane space (Gomkale2021Mapping).

TOMM22 serves as a receptor for the proapoptotic protein Bax, facilitating its translocation to mitochondria and playing a significant role in Bax-mediated apoptosis. This interaction is specific and is inhibited in cells with reduced TOMM22 expression (Bellot2006TOM22). TOMM22 also interacts with the mitochondrial BK Ca channel, indicating its involvement in the import pathway of mitoBK Ca (Zhang2017The).

Phosphorylation of TOMM22 by protein kinase CSNK2/CK2 is crucial for mitophagy in mammalian skeletal muscle. This modification affects its interaction with PINK1, a key regulator of mitochondrial quality control, enhancing PINK1's binding to TOMM22 and promoting mitophagy (Kravic2018In).
