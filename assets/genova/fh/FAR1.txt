# FAR1

## Overview
The FAR1 gene encodes the enzyme fatty acyl-CoA reductase 1, a crucial component in lipid metabolism. This enzyme is primarily involved in the biosynthesis of ether lipids, such as plasmalogens, by catalyzing the reduction of fatty acyl-CoAs to fatty alcohols. Plasmalogens are significant constituents of cellular membranes, particularly in tissues like the central nervous system, heart, and kidneys. The FAR1 protein is a peroxisomal membrane protein, characterized as a tail-anchored protein, and plays a pivotal role as a rate-limiting enzyme in ether lipid biosynthesis. Its activity is tightly regulated by plasmalogen levels through a feedback mechanism that modulates protein degradation. Mutations in the FAR1 gene can lead to various neurological and developmental disorders, underscoring its importance in maintaining cellular lipid homeostasis (Honsho2013Topogenesis; Ferdinandusse2021An).

## Structure
The FAR1 protein is a peroxisomal membrane protein involved in the biosynthesis of ether lipids, specifically plasmalogens. It is a tail-anchored protein consisting of 515 amino acids. The primary structure includes an N-terminal domain (amino acids 1-465) facing the cytosol, a transmembrane domain (amino acids 466-483), and a C-terminal domain (amino acids 484-515) located in the peroxisomal matrix (Ferdinandusse2021An). The transmembrane region is crucial for the protein's function and regulation, particularly in the feedback mechanism involving plasmalogen levels (Ferdinandusse2021An).

The FAR1 protein's secondary and tertiary structures are not explicitly detailed in the available context. However, the transmembrane domain suggests a structural role in anchoring the protein within the peroxisomal membrane. The quaternary structure is not described, indicating that FAR1 may function as a monomer or its multimeric state is not yet characterized.

The protein's activity is regulated by protein degradation in response to increased plasmalogen levels, suggesting a feedback mechanism that is not fully understood (Ferdinandusse2021An). Specific post-translational modifications or splice variant isoforms are not mentioned in the context provided.

## Function
The FAR1 gene encodes the enzyme fatty acyl-CoA reductase 1, which plays a crucial role in lipid metabolism by catalyzing the reduction of fatty acyl-CoAs to fatty alcohols. This enzymatic activity is essential for the biosynthesis of ether lipids, including plasmalogens, which are important components of cellular membranes, particularly in the central nervous system, heart, kidney, and white blood cells (Honsho2013Topogenesis; Ferdinandusse2021An). FAR1 is localized to the peroxisomal membrane, where it acts as a rate-limiting enzyme in the biosynthesis of ether (phospho)lipids (Ferdinandusse2021An).

In healthy human cells, FAR1 activity is regulated by plasmalogen levels through a feedback mechanism. When plasmalogen levels are high, FAR1 activity is downregulated by increased degradation of the FAR1 protein, ensuring controlled synthesis of ether lipids and maintaining cellular lipid homeostasis (Ferdinandusse2021An). The enzyme's activity is crucial for the production of fatty alcohols, such as hexadecanol and octadecanol, which are used in the formation of 1-alkyldihydroxyacetone phosphate (1-alkyl-DHAP), a precursor in ether lipid biosynthesis (Buchert2014A; Ferdinandusse2021An). This process is vital for maintaining normal cellular lipid metabolism and function.

## Clinical Significance
Mutations in the FAR1 gene are associated with several neurological and developmental disorders. De novo variants in FAR1, specifically at the p.Arg480 position, lead to an autosomal dominant neurological disorder characterized by spastic paraparesis, bilateral congenital or juvenile cataracts, speech and gross motor developmental delay, and truncal hypotonia. These mutations disrupt the plasmalogen-dependent feedback regulation of FAR1 protein levels, resulting in uncontrolled ether lipid production (Ferdinandusse2021An). 

In contrast, FAR1 deficiency caused by biallelic variants results in defective ether lipid synthesis and plasmalogen deficiency. This condition is linked to severe intellectual disability, epilepsy, microcephaly, congenital cataracts, growth retardation, and spasticity. The mutations lead to a loss of FAR1's reductase activity, essential for converting fatty acyl-CoAs to fatty alcohols in plasmalogen biosynthesis (Buchert2014A).

Alterations in FAR1 expression or function can also impact cellular lipid homeostasis, potentially contributing to other disorders such as Sjögren-Larsson syndrome, which is characterized by ichthyosis, neurological symptoms, and intellectual disability (Honsho2013Topogenesis). These findings highlight the critical role of FAR1 in lipid metabolism and its implications in various genetic disorders.
