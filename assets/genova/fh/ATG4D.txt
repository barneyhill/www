# ATG4D

## Overview
ATG4D is a gene that encodes the autophagy related 4D cysteine peptidase, a member of the ATG4 family of cysteine proteases. This protein plays a pivotal role in the autophagy process, a cellular mechanism essential for degrading and recycling cytoplasmic components. The ATG4D protein is involved in the processing of Atg8-related proteins, such as GABARAP-L1, which is crucial for autophagosome formation and maturation. It is characterized by a central C54 peptidase domain and a putative Bcl-2 homology-3 (BH3) domain, suggesting its involvement in both autophagy and apoptosis pathways (Betin2009Caspase; Kyöstilä2015A). The protein's activity is regulated by caspase-3 cleavage, which enhances its function and links it to mitochondrial targeting and cell death pathways (Betin2009Atg4D). Mutations in the ATG4D gene have been associated with neurodegenerative and neurodevelopmental disorders, highlighting its significance in maintaining neuronal health and cellular homeostasis (Morimoto2023Biallelic; Kyöstilä2015A).

## Structure
The ATG4D protein is a member of the ATG4 family of cysteine proteases, which are involved in the autophagy process. The primary structure of ATG4D includes a central C54 peptidase domain, which is crucial for its enzymatic activity (Kyöstilä2015A). The N-terminal region contains a PEST sequence, a caspase site, and a cryptic mitochondrial target sequence, while the C-terminal region holds a putative Bcl-2 homology-3 (BH3) domain (Kyöstilä2015A). 

The protein's tertiary structure is not fully detailed, but it is known that the N-terminal region of ATG4D can autoinhibit its activity by covering the catalytic site, similar to the mechanism observed in ATG4B (Betin2009Atg4D). Caspase-3 cleavage removes this autoinhibitory domain, enhancing ATG4D's activity (Betin2009Atg4D). 

ATG4D shares significant sequence similarity with ATG4B, including a conserved catalytic triad (Cys 134 /Asp 356 /His 358), which is essential for its protease function (Li2011Kinetics). The absence of detailed crystal structures for ATG4D limits the understanding of its quaternary structure, but modeling based on ATG4B provides some insights (TamargoGómez2021ATG4D).

## Function
The ATG4D gene encodes a cysteine protease that plays a crucial role in the autophagy process, a cellular mechanism for degrading and recycling cytoplasmic components. In healthy human cells, ATG4D is involved in the regulation of autophagy by processing Atg8-related proteins, such as GABARAP-L1, facilitating their lipidation and delipidation, which are essential for autophagosome formation and maturation (Betin2009Caspase). This activity is critical for maintaining cellular homeostasis, particularly under stress conditions like nutrient deprivation (Kyöstilä2015A).

ATG4D is also implicated in apoptosis, as its cleavage by caspase-3 enhances its activity, linking it to mitochondrial targeting and cell death pathways (Betin2009Caspase). The protein is recruited to mitochondria, suggesting a role in mitochondrial autophagy (mitophagy) or in response to mitochondrial damage (Betin2009Caspase). In the context of cellular stress, ATG4D-mediated autophagy protects cells from apoptosis, highlighting its protective role during cellular stress (Betin2009Caspase). The protein's activity is regulated by proteasomal degradation, ensuring its turnover and function in cellular homeostasis (Betin2009Caspase).

## Clinical Significance
Mutations in the ATG4D gene have been linked to various neurodegenerative and neurodevelopmental disorders. In dogs, a missense variant in ATG4D, specifically c.1288G>A, results in a neurodegenerative vacuolar storage disease characterized by cerebellar ataxia and altered autophagy pathways. This mutation leads to progressive cerebellar degeneration and vacuolar changes in tissues, highlighting the gene's role in maintaining neuronal health (Kyöstilä2015A). 

In humans, bi-allelic variants in ATG4D are associated with a neurodevelopmental disorder marked by speech and motor impairments. These variants, which include missense and frameshift mutations, affect conserved amino acid residues and are predicted to impair protein functionality rather than expression levels. This impairment is linked to altered autophagosome biogenesis, suggesting a disruption in autophagy-related processes (Morimoto2023Biallelic). 

The clinical significance of ATG4D mutations is further underscored by their potential role in non-canonical autophagy and their connection to mitochondria, which may be critical for understanding the pathogenesis of related disorders (Morimoto2023Biallelic). These findings emphasize the importance of ATG4D in both neuronal development and maintenance.

## Interactions
ATG4D interacts with several proteins, primarily within the context of autophagy and apoptosis. It is involved in the processing of GABARAP-L1, a member of the Atg8 family, through its enzymatic activity, which is enhanced upon cleavage by caspase-3. This cleavage activates ATG4D, allowing it to prime and delipidate GABARAP-L1, suggesting a specific interaction between these proteins (Betin2009Caspase). The interaction with GABARAP-L1 is distinct from its interaction with LC3, another autophagy-related protein, as ATG4D does not exhibit significant activity towards LC3 (Betin2009Caspase).

ATG4D also has a putative C-terminal BH3 domain, which may facilitate interactions with Bcl-2 family proteins, linking it to apoptotic pathways (Betin2009Caspase). This domain suggests a potential role in mitochondrial targeting and apoptosis, as ATG4D is recruited to mitochondria during apoptosis (Betin2009Caspase).

In the context of neurodegeneration, ATG4D interacts with GABARAP, influencing GABA A receptor trafficking. In ATG4D-deficient mice, there is an increased interaction between GABARAP and the γ2 subunit of the GABA A receptor, affecting receptor localization and function (TamargoGómez2021ATG4D).
